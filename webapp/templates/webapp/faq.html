No, this dashboard tracks Visa Bulletin cutoff date movements only, not PERM processing times.

Here's the difference:

  • PERM Processing Time (not tracked here): How long it takes the Department of Labor (DOL) to approve your labor certification. Currently 6-12 months depending on audit status.
  • Visa Bulletin Wait (what this dashboard tracks): Once your PERM is approved, your PERM filing date becomes your "priority date." The Visa Bulletin publishes monthly cutoff dates, and this dashboard shows how those dates have moved historically and projects when your priority date might become current.

For PERM processing times, check DOL's PERM statistics or trackitt.com timelines.

  • Final Action Dates: When your priority date reaches this cutoff, USCIS can approve your green card (I-485).
  • Dates for Filing: When your priority date reaches this cutoff, you can submit your I-485 application (Adjustment of Status), but it won't be approved until the Final Action date is also current.

In most cases, people watch Final Action Dates as they determine when you actually get the green card. Filing Dates are useful if you want to file early to get work authorization (EAD) while waiting.

Your priority date is the date when your immigration petition was filed:

  • Family-Sponsored: The date your sponsor filed the I-130 petition.
  • Employment-Based: The date your PERM labor certification was filed (or I-140 if PERM-exempt).

The Visa Bulletin publishes monthly "cutoff dates." If your priority date is earlier than the cutoff, your case can move forward. This dashboard tracks how those cutoffs have moved over time.

The projections are rough estimates based on the average movement speed from the last 12 months of Visa Bulletin data. They assume:

  • Historical trends will continue at the same pace.
  • No major policy changes or demand shifts.
  • No retrogressions or sudden jumps.

Important: Visa Bulletin movements are affected by many unpredictable factors (Congress, USCIS policy, demand fluctuations). Use these projections as a rough guide, not a guarantee.

The dashboard updates monthly with official data from the U.S. Department of State.

Retrogression happens when demand for visas exceeds the annual quota. The State Department moves cutoff dates backward to slow down visa issuance.

Common causes:

  • Surge in new applications
  • USCIS policy changes accelerating approvals
  • End of fiscal year adjustments

You can see historical retrogressions on the chart (when the line dips downward). This helps you understand if your visa category is prone to volatility.

Parents, spouses, and unmarried children under 21 of U.S. citizens are Immediate Relatives (IR) and are not subject to Visa Bulletin quotas.

They don't appear in this dashboard because:

  • There are no monthly cutoff dates to track.
  • Visas are immediately available once USCIS approves the petition.
  • Processing depends only on USCIS workload, not the Visa Bulletin.

This dashboard only tracks Family Preference categories (F1, F2A, F2B, F3, F4) and Employment-Based categories (EB1-EB5), which have annual quotas.

The dashboard automatically refreshes daily at 9 AM UTC to check for new Visa Bulletins from the State Department.

The official Visa Bulletin is typically published around the 9th-15th of each month for the following month (e.g., the December bulletin is published in mid-November).

All data comes directly from travel.state.gov.
